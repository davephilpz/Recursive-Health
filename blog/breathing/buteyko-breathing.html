1. What is butekyo?

The Buteyko breathing technique is a method that was developed by Ukrainian doctor Konstantin Buteyko in the 1950s. It's based on the premise that numerous medical conditions, including asthma and other respiratory disorders, are worsened or even caused by chronically hyperventilating or overbreathing.

According to Buteyko's theory, overbreathing leads to a decrease in carbon dioxide (CO2) levels in the blood, which in turn disrupts the body's ability to distribute oxygen effectively due to the altered pH and the Bohr effect. CO2 is not merely a waste gas; it plays a vital role in the body, including the regulation of pH, dilating airways and blood vessels, and facilitating the release of oxygen to tissues by hemoglobin.

The Buteyko method teaches people to breathe more slowly and shallowly through their noses rather than their mouths, with the aim of increasing CO2 levels to improve the efficiency of the respiratory system and overall health. The practices include a series of reduced-breathing exercises that help to normalize breathing volume.

The Buteyko technique has been used by some people as a complementary approach to managing conditions like asthma, anxiety, and sleep disturbances such as snoring and sleep apnea. However, it is essential to note that while some find Buteyko breathing beneficial, the method should not replace conventional treatment prescribed by a healthcare provider, especially for serious conditions like asthma. Instead, it should be used in consultation with a healthcare provider.

Possible buteyko exercises

  • Monitoring breath holding time as a measure of CO2 tolerance
  • Gradual reduction of breath volume using a series of controlled exercises.
  • Using breath holds and shallow breathing to address acute symptoms.

2. What is the Wim Hoff breathing method

The Wim Hof Method, developed by Wim Hof, also known as "The Iceman," is a combination of meditation, breathing exercises, and cold exposure training that is designed to teach people how to control their bodies to improve health and enhance physical and mental performance.

The Wim Hof breathing method specifically involves a cycle of controlled hyperventilation followed by breath-holding that's claimed to influence the autonomic nervous system and the body's immune response

Main factors of the Wim Hoff breathing technique

  • Controlled Hyperventilation: This is a series of 30 to 40 deep, forceful breaths. You inhale fully (but not to the point of strain) and exhale without fully forcing the air out. The goal is to saturate the body with oxygen and induce a state of hyperoxia, or higher-than-normal oxygen levels.
  • Exhalation and Breath Retention: After the last breath in the hyperventilation series, you exhale normally and then hold your breath for as long as you can without force. This breath retention phase is when carbon dioxide levels increase and oxygen utilization by the body becomes more efficient.
  • Recovery Breath: Once you can no longer hold your breath, you take a deep breath in and hold it for another 10 to 15 seconds before releasing it.

This cycle is typically repeated for three to four rounds. Practitioners of the method report feelings of euphoria, calm, and increased tolerance to cold temperatures after performing these breathing exercises.
