1. Types of exercise

Firstly, exercise-induced hormesis refers to the beneficial adaptations that occur in response to the physiological stress imposed by physical exercise. This hormetic response is a fundamental principle in exercise physiology, underlying the improvements in health and physical performance associated with regular physical activity.

There are two main types of exercise, but each of these can also be broken down into many sub types focused on strengthening different muscles, nerves and bodily functions.

  1. Aerobic
    1. Zone 1: Active Recovery (50-60% of MHR)
      • Engage in low-intensity activities like walking, light cycling, or a leisurely swim.
      • Use a heart rate monitor to ensure you're staying within 50-60% of your MHR.
      • Focus on activities that feel very easy and don't cause any strain.
    2. Zone 2: Endurance (60-70% of MHR)
      • Engage in low-intensity activities like walking, light cycling, or a leisurely swim.
      • Use a heart rate monitor to ensure you're staying within 50-60% of your MHR.
      • Focus on activities that feel very easy and don't cause any strain.
  2. Anaerobic means that it is performed without oxygen.

2. 5 ways exercise makes you stronger

Stress is all bad for your health, right? Yes and no. While all stress causes damage to your body, some if it can trigger natural defenses in your body. Your body takes this stress stimulus and becomes stronger due to this. This is called hormesis and these are positive stressors . The most well known form of hormesis is probably exercise. Lift a weight and become stronger, run and gain more endurance, etc.

  1. Muscle Hypertrophy and Strength:
    • Resistance Training: Causes micro-tears in muscle fibers, leading to their repair and growth (muscle hypertrophy).
    • Protein Synthesis: Exercise stimulates protein synthesis pathways, crucial for muscle repair and growth.
  2. Cardiovascular Adaptations:
    • Endurance Training: Improves heart function and increases the efficiency of oxygen utilization in muscles.
    • Capillarization: Increases the number of capillaries in the muscle tissue, enhancing blood flow and oxygen delivery.
  3. Metabolic Efficiency:
    • Improved Insulin Sensitivity: Regular exercise enhances the body's ability to use insulin, reducing the risk of type 2 diabetes.
    • Increased Mitochondrial Density: Exercise, especially aerobic, increases the number and efficiency of mitochondria in cells, boosting energy production.
  4. Neurological Benefits:
    • Neurogenesis: Exercise has been shown to promote the growth of new neurons in certain brain regions.
    • PIncreased Neurotrophic Factors: Exercise elevates levels of brain-derived neurotrophic factor (BDNF) and other growth factors, supporting brain health.
  5. Hormonal Responses:
    • Endorphin Release: Exercise can lead to an increase in endorphins, improving mood and pain tolerance.
    • Stress Hormones: Physical activity modulates the release of cortisol and other stress hormones, enhancing stress resilience.

3. The 5 main benefits of exercise on a high level

Exercise provides numerous benefits as outlined in the previous section on hormesis, but the five main benefits of exercise can be summarized into the following categories.

  1. Cardiovascular Health: Reduced risk of heart diseases, lower blood pressure, and improved cholesterol levels.
  2. Muscle Strength and Endurance: Enhanced muscle mass, strength, and endurance, aiding in daily activities and reducing the risk of falls in older adults.
  3. Metabolic Health: Better glucose control, improved lipid profiles, and reduced risk of metabolic syndrome.
  4. Mental Health: Improved cognitive function, reduced symptoms of depression and anxiety, and better overall mental well-being.
  5. Longevity and Aging: Exercise has been linked to increased lifespan and a delay in the onset of age-related diseases.
