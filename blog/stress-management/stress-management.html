Using Stress to Your Advantage

How to manage your stress to get stronger mentally and physically

Article Overview

Learn about types of stress, how they affect your body and how to use them to your advantage or get rid of them.

  1. What is stress?
  2. Positive vs. negative stressors?
  3. Types of positive stressors (hormesis)
  4. Types of negative stressors (anti-hormesis)

1. What is stress anyways?

When people hear stress, they automatically think about their job, relationship, money situation or other such things. This is however just mental stress, and is only the tip of the iceberg. Mental stress can manifest into physical stress as hormones and other chemicals released in the brain controls functions throughout the body.

Stress comes in many shapes and forms and is by no means limited to just mental stress. Our environment, situation, lifestyle choices, relationships and more can also cause either physical or mental stress on our bodies.

This all sounds really horrible, but there is light at the end of the tunnel. There are positive stressors known as hormesis and we will breakdown the types as well as list out positive and negative stressors.

2. Positive vs. negative stress: What is the difference?

Stress is all bad for your health, right? Yes and no. While all stress causes damage to your body, some if it can trigger natural defenses in your body. Your body takes this stress stimulus and becomes stronger due to this. This is called hormesis and these are positive stressors . The most well known form of hormesis is probably exercise. Lift a weight and become stronger, run and gain more endurance, etc.

There are negative stressors too that only cause harm to your body with little to no benefits; in other words, you will lose more than you gain. These include things like cigarette smoke, a polluted environment, processed foods, too much sugar, poor relationships, a rough job environment etc.

Managing stress

Too much stress is bad, even if it is a positive stressor and therefore stress management is paramount.

How do you manage stress? The answer is simple, eliminate all negative stressors and balance positive stressors with ample rest and recovery. Sounds simple, right? Some stresses may be out of your control and there are ways in the breathing section to forcefully calm your body, but for all of the rest, you are in full control.

Chronic vs. acute stress

Chronic stress is at the root of most diseases also known as lifestyle illnesses. A lifestyle illness is something caused 100% by a person's lifestyle choices and has nothing to do with genetics. Many experts say that 80-90% of all disease is due to poor lifestyle choices. This is both good and bad news. Good in that you can easily stop it, and bad in that some habits are not so easily broken. A chronic stress means that there is no period of healing for the body to perform its job and repair. Any type of chronic stress should be avoided at all costs.

Acute stress is a stress that is situational and happens based on the environment such as extreme hot or cold, or is a personal choice. As long as acute stress such as exercise is accompanied by ample rest and recovery, you will be stronger than you were before. the old saying: "what doesn't kill you makes you stronger" couldn't be more true.

3. 10 Types of negative stressors (anti-hormesis) with a net loss

The following list of 10 types of anti-hormesis should be avoided if possible as they will not provide any benefit or boost your health even if done in moderation and with proper rest and recovery. Small doses of them may provide some benefit, but it has yet to be proven by science. Will make future updates if I see any findings in medical journals etc.

  1. Low-Dose Toxicity: Substances that are unexpectedly more toxic at lower doses.
  2. Reverse Drug Efficacy: Drugs that have adverse effects at low doses but beneficial effects at higher doses.
  3. Negative Radiation Effects: Instances where low levels of radiation cause more harm than higher levels.
  4. Environmental Stressors: Low levels of environmental stressors that lead to negative biological effects.
  5. Chemical Sensitivity: Increased sensitivity to chemicals at lower doses.
  6. Immunological Response: Weakened or adverse immune responses triggered by low levels of pathogens or allergens.
  7. Nutritional Deficiency: Situations where lower intake of certain nutrients leads to more severe health issues than complete absence.
  8. Microbial Tolerance: Microbes developing tolerance or resistance at low levels of antibiotics or antifungals.
  9. Psychological Stressors: Minor stressors causing disproportionately large negative psychological effects.
  10. Sub-therapeutic Dosing: Lower than necessary dosing of a medication leading to adverse effects.
